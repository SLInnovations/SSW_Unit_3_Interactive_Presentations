What Is a Conjunction?
Conjunctions are connecting words.
A conjunction is a word that connects (joins) words, phrases, or sentences.
Without a conjunction:
"I like dogs. I like cats."
Two separate ideas — a bit choppy
With a conjunction:
"I like dogs and cats."
Ideas connected smoothly
Conjunctions can connect: words, phrases, or whole sentences.
Learn
Meet the FANBOYS
Seven coordinating conjunctions — one easy acronym.
FFor= because, since (gives a reason)
AAnd= also, in addition (adds information)
NNor= and not, neither (negative addition)
BBut= however, on the other hand (shows contrast)
OOr= alternative, choice (gives options)
YYet= but, despite that (unexpected contrast)
SSo= therefore, as a result (shows result)

Tricky
Tricky Conjunctions
Watch out for these common mix-ups.
🤔 BUT vs. YET — What's the Difference?
Both show contrast, but YET has a stronger sense of surprise.
"He is small but strong." — Simple contrast.
"The hike was exhausting, yet everyone wanted to go again." — Surprising contrast.
🤔 FOR vs. SO — Cause or Result?
FOR gives the reason (looks backward). SO gives the result (looks forward).
"She brought an umbrella, for it looked like rain." — The reason she brought it.
"It started raining, so she opened her umbrella." — The result of the rain.
🤔 NOR — The Tricky One
NOR is used in negative sentences to mean "and not." The word order flips after NOR.
"She doesn't like broccoli, nor does she like spinach."
Notice: "does she" flips to question order after NOR.
